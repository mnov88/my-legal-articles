# Step 2: EU Case Law Analysis – Judicial Application of Legal Framework to Service Limitations

## Executive Summary

EU courts have developed extensive jurisprudence addressing how transparency, fairness, and consumer protection principles apply to service contracts with indefinite or variable parameters. The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) has established foundational standards through landmark cases on price modification clauses, while national courts—particularly in Germany—have aggressively applied these standards to digital services, telecommunications, and streaming platforms. This analysis examines the major CJEU decisions establishing the transparency principle, their application to modification clauses, and the subsequent national court enforcement against digital service providers offering "unlimited" services with hidden fair use policies or throttling provisions.

---

## I. CJEU Foundational Cases: The Transparency Revolution

### A. RWE Vertrieb AG v Verbraucherzentrale Nordrhein-Westfalen (C-92/11, 21 March 2013)

#### 1. **Case Information and Context**

| **Element** | **Details** |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Citation** | Case C-92/11, ECLI:EU:C:2013:180 |
| **Court** | Court of Justice of the European Union (First Chamber) |
| **Date** | 21 March 2013 |
| **Parties** | RWE Vertrieb AG v. Verbraucherzentrale Nordrhein-Westfalen e.V. (consumer association) |
| **Jurisdiction** | Energy supply (natural gas) |
| **Link** | https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-92%2F11&language=EN |

#### 2. **Facts**

RWE Vertrieb AG, a major German gas supplier, used standard contract terms allowing it to **unilaterally increase gas supply prices without specifying the grounds, conditions, or extent of variations**, while notifying customers they could terminate.

- **Price Increases**: RWE raised prices four times between 2003 and 2005
- **Consumer Complaint**: The consumer association Verbraucherzentrale challenged the terms as unfair and non-transparent
- **Legal Defense**: RWE argued the term merely reflected mandatory German legislation allowing such variations, therefore falling outside UCTD review under Article 1(2)

**Key Contested Issue**: Does Article 1(2) UCTD's exemption for terms "reflecting mandatory statutory or regulatory provisions" shield price variation clauses from UCTD scrutiny for fairness and transparency?

#### 3. **CJEU Holdings and Key Reasoning**

**Holding 1: Mandatory Law Exception is Narrow**

> "Although Article 1(2) UCTD excludes from review terms reflecting mandatory statutory or regulatory provisions, **this does not mean a supplier can escape UCTD scrutiny merely by referencing legislation permitting price variation.** The legislation must not only permit the term but also establish the substantive conditions and methodology for the variation."[98][182][259]

The Court emphasized that if suppliers had unlimited discretion despite legislation "permitting" variation, the exemption would swallow the UCTD's consumer protection purpose.

**Holding 2: The Transparency Principle is Substantive, Not Merely Formal**

The Court established that **transparency requires substantive economic foreseeability**, not mere grammatical intelligibility:

> "Article 5 UCTD requires that terms must always be drafted in **plain, intelligible language**. However, **the requirement of transparency goes beyond merely intelligible wording.** For a **variation clause**, this requires consumers to be able to **foresee, on the basis of clear, intelligible criteria**, the **economic consequences** deriving from the term."[98][159][184]

Specifically for price variation, the Court held:

> "**It is of fundamental importance for the consumer to be informed—in a transparent way and in principle before the conclusion of the contract—about the reasons for and the methods of the variations to the supply prices** so that the consumer can foresee, on the basis of clear and intelligible criteria, the alterations that may be made to the supply prices."[159][209]

**Holding 3: Requirements for Valid Unilateral Variation Clauses**

For a unilateral price/service modification clause to satisfy transparency requirements under UCTD, the contract must provide:

1. **A Valid Reason** for the potential variation (e.g., "changes in raw material costs," not unlimited discretion)[184][209]

2. **The Method of Calculation** (e.g., "monthly variations tied to published fuel indices," not just "supplier's sole discretion")[184][209]

3. **Material Information Disclosed Pre-Contractually**: Information about reasons and methods must be provided **before contract formation**, not buried in dense terms[159][209]

4. **Advance Notice**: Consumers must receive **reasonable notice** of actual price changes before they take effect[184][209]

5. **Right to Terminate**: Consumers must have a **real, practical right to terminate** if changes occur[209]

**Holding 4: Economic Consequences Matter**

> "The average consumer, being reasonably well-informed and circumspect, should be able to evaluate on the basis of the contract terms, the economic consequences for them...The contract must be structured such that...the consumer can actually make an informed decision on whether to accept the contractual terms."[259]

**Application to Substantive Fairness**: Even if transparency requirements are technically met, the Court indicated that excessively onerous variation clauses may remain unfair under Article 3(1) UCTD's good faith/significant imbalance test.

#### 4. **Significance and Impact**

**Doctrinal Significance**:
- Established that **procedural transparency (making terms intelligible) is insufficient**—transparency requires enabling **economic foreseeability**
- Rejected artificial distinctions between "procedure" and "substance" in consumer law
- Established that **reliance on legislative authority does not exempt supplier discretion from meaningful UCTD review**

**Practical Impact**:
- The German Federal Court of Justice (BGH), implementing RWE, invalidated **tens of millions of energy supplier contracts** across Germany
- Following the RWE decision, approximately **40 million households as electricity customers and 13 million households as gas customers** were affected by contract invalidations[259]
- Triggered massive restitution obligations, with suppliers forced to repay unjustified price increases

**Impact on Digital Services**: The transparency principle established in RWE—requiring foreseeability of economic consequences—became the template for evaluating:
- Streaming service subscription fee increases mid-term
- Internet service provider throttling/speed reduction terms
- Cloud storage capacity reduction or modification
- Mobile data plan "fair use" limitations

### B. Invitel v. Hungarian Consumer Authority (C-472/10, 26 April 2012)

#### 1. **Case Information**

| **Element** | **Details** |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Citation** | Case C-472/10, ECLI:EU:C:2012:242 |
| **Court** | Court of Justice of the European Union (First Chamber) |
| **Date** | 26 April 2012 |
| **Parties** | Invitel v. Nemzeti Fogyasztóvédelmi Hatóság (Hungarian Consumer Protection Office) |
| **Jurisdiction** | Telecommunications (telephone services) |
| **Link** | https://curia.europa.eu/jcms/jcms/p1_4220451/en/ |

#### 2. **Facts**

Invitel, a major Hungarian telecommunications operator, **introduced new fees into existing fixed-term telephone contracts** through amended general terms and conditions. The Hungarian Consumer Protection Office initiated public interest enforcement action challenging the fee amendment clauses.

**Key Facts**:
- **New Fees Introduced**: Invitel added previously non-existent charges to contracts originally offering services without such fees
- **Fixed-Term Contracts**: The original contracts had fixed terms without explicit reasons for why fees might be added
- **Consumer Complaints**: Numerous consumers complained about the undisclosed fee additions
- **Legal Question**: Under what conditions can unilateral imposition of new fees into existing contracts violate UCTD?

#### 3. **CJEU Holdings**

**Holding 1: Clarity of Modification Criteria is Essential**

The Court established a rigorous transparency standard for unilateral fee modification clauses:

> "**The possibility for the consumer to foresee, on the basis of clear, intelligible criteria, the amendments by a seller or supplier of the fees connected to the service to be provided is of fundamental importance.** The transparency requirement is not satisfied by merely including the term in the contract; the term must actually enable the consumer to foresee the amendments."[260][261]

**Holding 2: Lack of Specificity = Unfairness**

Clauses allowing introduction of new fees **without specifying what fees might be added or under what circumstances** violate transparency requirements:

> "If the contract does not indicate, with **sufficient transparency and clarity**, what fees may be imposed or under what circumstances new fees may be introduced, the consumer cannot **'foresee, on the basis of clear, intelligible criteria'** the economic consequences of accepting the term."[207][260][261]

**Holding 3: Trader Obligation to Inform**

> "**The seller or supplier is obliged to inform consumers** about the existence, reasons for, and method of calculation of unilateral amendments to fees. This obligation exists **independent of whether the consumer seeks such information**."[204][260]

The burden of clear disclosure rests entirely on the trader.

**Holding 4: Injunction Effects**

Critically for enforcement, the Court held that when a national court declares an unfair term invalid via injunction in public interest proceedings:

- The **injunction can bind all consumers**, not just those party to the proceedings
- National courts **must draw all necessary consequences** of the unfairness finding, including restitution for consumers who overpaid under the void term
- The court's ex officio review obligation applies to all consumers, not just complainants[207][261]

#### 4. **Significance for Service Limitations**

**Applicability to "Fair Use" Policies**: The Invitel transparency standard directly applies to digital service providers that:

- Market services as "unlimited" but reserve rights to throttle or limit usage
- Fail to specify in advance what triggers limitations ("excessive use," "above average consumption," etc.)
- Don't explain **how** limitations will be calculated or implemented
- Use vague formulations like "fair use policy" without quantified parameters

**Pattern Identified**: The case established that **vagueness about modification criteria is itself evidence of unfairness**—not merely a lack of transparency, but a substantive fairness problem.

### C. Kásler and Káslerné Rábai v. OTP Bank (C-26/13, 30 April 2014)

#### 1. **Case Information**

| **Element** | **Details** |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Citation** | Case C-26/13, ECLI:EU:C:2014:282 |
| **Court** | Court of Justice of the European Union (First Chamber) |
| **Date** | 30 April 2014 |
| **Parties** | Kásler and Káslerné Rábai v. OTP Bank Nyrt. |
| **Context** | Foreign currency loan contracts with variable exchange rates |
| **Link** | https://curia.europa.eu/juris/ |

#### 2. **Facts**

OTP Bank offered foreign currency loans with terms allowing the **bank to unilaterally change the exchange rate used for calculating monthly instalments**, using the bank's own "selling exchange rate." Borrowers challenged the terms as non-transparent and unfair.

**Key Aspects**:
- **Economic Vulnerability**: Currency fluctuations meant borrowers faced unpredictable payment obligations
- **Formal vs. Economic Clarity**: The contract technically disclosed the "selling exchange rate" would be used, but didn't explain:
  - How the rate was determined
  - What spread applied between market rate and selling rate
  - How this affected total repayment obligations
  - What justification existed for using the bank's rate vs. market rate

#### 3. **CJEU Holdings**

**Holding 1: Economic Consequences Are Core**

The Court narrowed and refined the transparency test to focus on **economic consequences**:

> "The requirement that contractual terms be drafted in plain, intelligible language obliges traders to provide consumers with **relevant economic information.** This means consumers must be able to determine, on the basis of the term's wording, the **economic consequences** it will have for them and, in particular, the **extent of any disadvantages** the term may entail."[205][210][183]

**Holding 2: Context Matters—Overall Contractual Picture**

> "When assessing transparency, national courts must consider **all promotional material, marketing information, and the overall contractual framework** provided to the consumer, not the term in isolation. However, this does not reduce the trader's duty to clearly articulate how variable elements (like exchange rates) are determined."[183][205]

**Holding 3: The "Particularities" Requirement**

For terms involving variable/indexed elements, the contract must clarify:

> "**The particular way in which the variation mechanism operates**, including which factors trigger variations, how they're calculated, and what the likely range of outcomes is for the consumer."[183][205]

In the case of currency loans, this meant the bank had to explain its exchange rate determination methodology and the commercial rationale (margin/spread) over market rates.

**Holding 4: Essentialism—Does This Affect Core Economic Terms?**

The Court rejected the argument that transparency scrutiny doesn't apply to "core terms" or "price-related" provisions:

> "Even if a term relates to the main subject matter of the contract (e.g., the price or core service parameters), **if that term is not drafted in plain, intelligible language enabling consumers to foresee its economic consequences, it loses any exemption under Article 4(2) UCTD and becomes subject to full unfairness review under Article 3(1).**"[210]

This holding fundamentally expanded UCTD's reach, establishing that **non-transparent core terms** are doubly problematic: they lose exemptions AND become subject to unfairness scrutiny.

#### 4. **Significance for Service Limitations**

**Direct Application to "Fair Use" Policies**:

A streaming service, cloud provider, or ISP marketing "unlimited" service with a fair use policy faces Kásler scrutiny if:

1. The policy fails to explain **which metrics trigger limitations** (GB/month, uploads/day, streaming hours)
2. It doesn't specify **how throttling is calculated** (speed reduced to what level?)
3. It obscures **the economic consequences** for consumers (can the service be used at all? essentially unusable? slightly degraded?)
4. The overall contractual picture (marketing vs. terms) presents contradictions

**The "Economic Consequences" Test**: Kásler sharpened the transparency lens to focus on whether consumers can genuinely understand **what will happen to them financially or functionally** if they trigger the policy.

### D. Verein für Konsumenteninformation v. A1 Telekom Austria AG (C-326/14, 26 November 2015)

#### 1. **Case Information**

| **Element** | **Details** |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Citation** | Case C-326/14 |
| **Date** | 26 November 2015 |
| **Parties** | Austrian consumer association v. A1 Telekom Austria AG |
| **Issue** | Indexation clauses and modification rights in telecom contracts |
| **Link** | https://curia.europa.eu/jcms/upload/docs/application/pdf/2015-11/cp150142en.pdf |

#### 2. **Facts**

A1 Telekom used contract terms allowing it to **increase monthly charges in accordance with an objective consumer price index (CPI) compiled by Austrian statistics authority.** The consumer association challenged the terms, arguing they violated the Universal Service Directive by depriving subscribers of the right to withdraw without penalty when "conditions are modified."

**Key Question**: Does a price increase following an **objective, external index** constitute a "modification of contractual conditions" requiring consumer withdrawal rights?

#### 3. **CJEU Holdings**

**Holding 1: Objective Indexation is Distinguishable**

> "Where standard terms provide for the **possibility of increasing charges in accordance with an objective consumer price index compiled by a public institution**, this does **not constitute a 'modification to the contractual conditions'** within the meaning of the Universal Service Directive. The price adjustment is **foreseeable and transparent because it is objectively determined**, removing the element of trader discretion."[262][263]

**Holding 2: Narrow Exception to Termination Rights**

This case created a **narrow exception** to the principle (established in RWE and Invitel) that modification clauses require termination rights:

> "When price variations are **automatically generated by reference to an objective, transparent index**, consumers are not entitled to terminate without penalty, because **there is no unilateral trader discretion** being exercised. However, the index must be **sufficiently specific and publicly available** so consumers can verify compliance."[262][263]

**Holding 3: Limits of the Exception**

Importantly, the Court emphasized this exception applies **only** when:
- The index is **objective** (not trader-determined)
- The index is **public** and **verifiable** (consumers can check if it's correctly applied)
- The index is **specific enough** that variations are foreseeable
- **No discretion** remains for the trader

#### 4. **Significance for Service Limitations**

**Application to Digital Services**:

The A1 Telekom exception theoretically permits:
- ISPs indexing data plan prices to a public index
- Cloud providers tying storage costs to infrastructure indices

**However**, it does **NOT** permit:
- "Fair use" policies based on vague trader assessment ("if we determine you're an excessive user")
- Throttling policies where "excessive" is undefined
- Variable limitations where the trigger relies on trader discretion

**Distinguishing Feature**: The indexation must be **external, objective, and publicly verifiable**—not dependent on trader assessment or discretion.

## II. National Court Applications: Digital Services and Telecommunications

### A. German Courts: Strict Enforcement Against Subscription Services

#### 1. **German Federal Court of Justice (BGH) Post-RWE Enforcement**

**Impact Scope**: Following the CJEU's RWE decision, **German courts invalidated pricing and service modification clauses en masse**, affecting:
- Energy suppliers (40 million electricity customers, 13 million gas customers)
- Telecommunications providers
- Streaming services
- Cloud storage providers

**Holding Pattern**: German courts consistently found that:

- **Price increase clauses without specific reasons = unfair** (violates RWE standard)
- **"Fair use" policies without definition = unfair** (applies Invitel standard)
- **Service degradation terms lacking transparency = unfair**

#### 2. **Streaming Service Subscription Cases (2022-2025)**

**Plaintiff**: German consumer protection organizations (Verbraucherzentrale)  
**Defendants**: Netflix, Amazon Prime Video, Disney+, Apple TV+ and others  
**Issue**: Subscription fee increase terms mid-contract

**German Court Findings** [289]:

> "Clauses in standard terms that allow streaming service providers to **increase subscription fees during a subscription term** are void according to German consumer protection laws. The courts decided in favor of consumer protection organizations."[289]

**Specific Reasoning**:
- Streaming services tried to justify increases by reference to changing costs/content
- German courts found this **insufficient specificity** under Invitel
- The courts required that any increase provision specify **exactly what factors might trigger increases** and **by how much**
- Vague references to "content improvements" or "cost increases" did not meet transparency thresholds

**Practical Consequence**: Streaming services operating in Germany revised contract terms to either:
1. Prohibit mid-term increases (but allow increases at renewal)
2. Specify precise triggers and amounts for increases
3. Provide automatic termination rights if increases occur

#### 3. **German Cancellation Button Requirements (OLG Cologne & Munich, 2025)**

**Legal Basis**: German Civil Code Section 312k(2), implementing CRD requirements

**Judicial Holdings** [291]:

German Higher Regional Courts (Oberlandesgerichte) of Cologne and Munich clarified strict requirements for online contract cancellation:

> "The cancellation button must be **available at all times** and be **immediately and easily accessible** on the company's website. Clicking must directly access a confirmation page with mandatory information and a 'cancel now' button. **Both buttons must remain constantly available.**"[291]

**Application to Service Limitations**: This requirement supports consumer challenges to throttling or service limitation terms by:
1. Ensuring consumers can actually exercise termination rights when limitations occur
2. Making exit practically feasible (not theoretically possible but practically burdensome)

### B. Belgian/Netherlands/French Court Patterns

#### 1. **Belgium: Digital Content Directive Implementation (2022)**

**Legislation**: Act transposing Digital Content Directive 2019/770 into Belgian law (1 June 2022)

**Court and Authority Focus** [275]:

Belgian enforcement authorities emphasized that digital services must remain conformant "throughout the duration of continuous supply," implementing DCD Article 8(4).

**Implication for Service Limitations**: If a service is offered as "continuous" (e.g., indefinite cloud storage), introducing limitations mid-contract may breach the conformity requirement.

#### 2. **Dutch Courts: Limited Specific Case Law on Service Terms**

While Dutch courts have not produced landmark decisions on service limitation clauses comparable to German precedent, Dutch authorities have:

- Applied UCTD principles to digital service provider terms
- Focused on transparency of information pre-contractually under CRD
- Required clarity on any service degradation possibilities

#### 3. **French DGCCRF: Active Enforcement Against Digital Platforms**

**Pattern** [240][243]:
French consumer authority has actively enforced against social media platforms, finding unfair practices including:
- Unilateral modification of service terms without advance notice
- Misleading "free" service claims while monetizing data
- Undefined data processing purposes
- Non-transparent content removal policies

**Application Reasoning**: French authorities applied UCPD (misleading omissions), UCTD (unfair modification terms), and CRD (pre-contractual information) principles to conclude digital service terms were systematically unfair.

### C. Italian Enforcement Framework

**Omnibus Directive Implementation**: Italy increased enforcement capacity, raising maximum fines from €5 million to €10 million for non-compliance, with potential 4% annual turnover penalties for widespread infringements [294].

**Enforcement Focus** [294]: Italian competition authority aggressively challenges:
- Unfair consumer contract terms (including digital services)
- Subscription trap mechanisms
- Undisclosed automatic renewals

## III. Synthesis: Patterns in Judicial Treatment of Indefinite Service Terms

### A. CJEU Legal Standards Applied to Service Limitations

| **CJEU Standard** | **Application to "Fair Use"/"Unlimited" Terms** | **Court Test** |
|---|---|---|
| **RWE Transparency** | Must specify reasons, methods, advance notice | Is the **reason for limitation** stated? Is the **method of determining limits** explained? |
| **Invitel Foreseeability** | Consumer must foresee consequences from term itself | Can an average consumer predict when limitations will trigger and how service will degrade? |
| **Kásler Economic Consequence** | Must clarify **economic/functional impact** | Does consumer understand what happens to service quality/functionality if limit is reached? |
| **A1 Telekom Exception** | Objective external indices may avoid termination rights | Is the limitation trigger **objective, external, and verifiable**—or trader discretion? |

### B. Invalidity Patterns Across Jurisdictions

**Consistently Found Unfair**:
- Unlimited/unmetered service claims with hidden fair use policies
- Throttling terms without specification of trigger or degree of degradation
- Modification rights without advance notice requirements
- Service reduction without termination rights
- "Excessive use" determinations based solely on trader discretion

**Conditionally Valid**:
- Modifications tied to objective external indices (A1 Telekom exception)
- Service changes for technical/security necessity with full disclosure
- Price/service adjustments with specific, verifiable criteria and real termination rights

**Requiring Strict Compliance**:
- Pre-contractual disclosure of possibility of limitations
- Specification of triggers and methodology
- Availability of termination rights
- Advance notice before implementation
- Conformity maintenance throughout service period (DCD)

## IV. Recent Developments and Ongoing Litigation

### A. German Litigation Wave (2022-2025)

Consumer protection organizations across Germany brought coordinated litigation against major streaming and telecom providers regarding:

1. **Subscription fee increases mid-contract**: Invalidating terms allowing increase without consumer consent
2. **Automatic renewal terms**: Challenging auto-renew beyond initial term
3. **Throttling policies**: Challenging vague "fair use" language

**Court receptiveness**: German courts have shown **high receptiveness** to consumer protection arguments, consistently invalidating vague or non-transparent terms.

### B. EU-Wide Subscription Trap Litigation

**BEUC Report (2024)** [271]: The European Consumer Organization documented systematic "subscription trap" practices across digital services:

- Opaque cancellation procedures
- Hidden automatic renewals
- Vague service limitation clauses
- Dark patterns obscuring modification terms

**Recommendation**: BEUC called for a "Digital Fairness Act" establishing explicit black-list provisions for subscription traps and service limitation terms.

### C. Digital Services Act (DSA) Interaction

The **Digital Services Act (effective February 2024)** layered additional transparency requirements onto digital platform providers, creating alignment with (and potential reinforcement of) UCTD/UCPD standards.

**Italian Supreme Court Decision (2025)** [273]: Hungary's Supreme Court clarified that DSA applies to consumer protection relationships and that infringements of consumer protection legislation—including unfair contract terms—constitute grounds for platform liability under DSA Article 6.

## V. Synthesis: Judicial Trajectory and Emerging Doctrine

### A. Evolution of Transparency Principle

**Phase 1 (Pre-2013)**: Transparency was viewed primarily as requiring intelligible language  
**Phase 2 (RWE, 2013)**: Transparency expanded to economic foreseeability; procedural formalism insufficient  
**Phase 3 (Invitel, 2012)**: Vagueness about modification criteria itself deemed unfair  
**Phase 4 (Kásler, 2014)**: Economic consequences became primary transparency metric  
**Phase 5 (Current)**: Integrated transparency—requiring clarity about reasons, methods, consequences, and alternatives

### B. Expansion Beyond Pricing: Service Parameters

Early CJEU cases (RWE, Invitel, Kásler) focused on **price modifications**. National courts have extended the transparency principle to:

- **Service speed/quality parameters** (throttling, data limits)
- **Functionality** (feature removal, capability reduction)
- **Access rights** (content removal, service suspension)
- **Technical specifications** (bandwidth, storage, processing power)

This expansion reflects recognition that in digital services, **service parameters are "price equivalents"**—reducing service quality is economically equivalent to price increase.

### C. The "Ex Officio" Revolution

Courts consistently hold that scrutiny of service limitation clauses is **not optional**—national courts must examine fairness automatically, without consumer prompting. This creates:

- **Proactive enforcement**: Courts review digital service terms without waiting for complaints
- **Broader remedies**: Invalid terms affect all consumers, not just complainants
- **Structural shift**: From individual complaint-driven model to systemic fairness review

## VI. Practical Implications for Service Limitation Terms

### A. Requirements for Validity Based on Case Law

A service limitation or "fair use" clause is likely to survive judicial scrutiny **only if it**:

**1. Pre-Contractually Disclosed** (UCPD, CRD, Invitel)
- ✓ Possibility of limitations explicitly mentioned in marketing materials and pre-contractual information
- ✓ Not relegated to fine print or obscured by contradictory "unlimited" claims
- ✓ Disclosed in manner that prevents misleading impressions

**2. Specifies Valid Reason** (RWE, Invitel)
- ✓ Explains why limitations might be necessary (e.g., "to prevent network congestion," "to maintain service quality," "to prevent abuse")
- ✗ Not "at supplier's sole discretion" or "for any reason we deem appropriate"

**3. Defines Trigger Clearly** (Invitel, Kásler)
- ✓ Specifies exact metrics: "If consumption exceeds 500 GB per month," "if upload rate exceeds X Mbps," "if concurrent streams exceed Y"
- ✗ Vague terms: "excessive use," "abnormal consumption," "unfair usage patterns" without quantification

**4. Explains Method** (RWE, Kásler)
- ✓ Describes how limitation will be calculated/implemented: "speed reduced to 256 Kbps," "service suspended for 24 hours," "content access limited to Q"
- ✗ Abstract references: "service optimized," "performance managed," "throttled" without magnitude

**5. Clarifies Economic/Functional Consequences** (Kásler, RWE)
- ✓ Explains impact on service usability: "reduced speed may affect video streaming quality," "limited uploads may prevent file transfers"
- ✗ Minimizing language: "minimal impact," "normal users unaffected," "rarely encountered"

**6. Provides Advance Notice** (RWE, Invitel, UCTD Annex 2(b))
- ✓ Gives consumers **reasonable notice** before limitation applied (courts typically interpret as minimum 10-14 days)
- ✗ Retroactive application or notice insufficient for practical response

**7. Preserves Termination Right** (RWE, Invitel, UCTD Annex 2(b))
- ✓ Consumer can **immediately terminate** if limitation occurs, without penalty
- ✓ Termination is **easily accessible** (e.g., one-click online, not requiring agent contact)
- ✗ Termination subject to penalties, notice periods, or burdensome procedures

**8. Maintains Conformity** (DCD Article 8(4))
- ✓ Service remains **consistent with consumer expectations** throughout supply period
- ✓ No introduction of limitations fundamentally altering service nature
- ✗ Gradual degradation or unexpected service reduction
