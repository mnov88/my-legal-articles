**Official Title:** Guidelines on Automated individual decision-making and Profiling for the purposes of Regulation 2016/679

**Document Number/Version:** WP251rev.01

**Adoption Date:** Adopted 3 October 2017; Last revised and adopted 6 February 2018

**Endorsement:** Endorsed by European Data Protection Board, 25 May 2018 (EDPB Endorsement 1/2018)

**Official Locations (with correct links):**

| Source | URL | Notes |
|--------|-----|-------|
| **EC Newsroom (Primary Official Source)** | `https://ec.europa.eu/newsroom/article29/redirection/document/49826` | PDF download available; direct EC official repository |
| **EDPB Official Guidelines Page** | `https://www.edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/our-documents/guidelines/automated-decision-making-and-profiling_en` | EDPB endorsement page with links to document versions |
| **PDPJ (Secondary Academic Repository)** | `https://www.pdpjournals.com/docs/887862.pdf` | Full text PDF hosted in academic repository |

**Key Content Covered (40 pages):**

- **Part I: Introduction and Background**
  - Risks of profiling: opacity, perpetuation of stereotypes, discrimination, lock-in effects
  - GDPR's regulatory approach

- **Part II: Definitions**
  - Article 4(4) GDPR: Definition of profiling with three-element analysis
  - Profiling categories: (i) general profiling; (ii) decision-making based on profiling; (iii) solely automated decision-making under Article 22

- **Part III: Article 22 Solely Automated Decision-Making**
  - Three constitutive conditions: (1) decision; (2) solely automated; (3) legal/significant effects
  - Three statutory exceptions: (a) contract necessity; (b) legal authorization; (c) explicit consent
  - Article 22(4) safeguards: information rights, human intervention, explanation, challenge rights

- **Part IV: General Profiling and Decision-Making Based on Profiling**
  - Article 5(1)(a) lawfulness, fairness, transparency principles
  - Article 5(1)(b) purpose limitation for further processing
  - Article 5(1)(c) data minimization
  - Transparency under Articles 13-15
  - Data subject rights (Articles 21, objection)
  - Special considerations for children, sensitive data
  - DPA role and DPIA requirements

- **Part V: Good Practice Recommendations**
  - Testing for bias and discrimination
  - Fairness audits
  - Documentation and accountability

### 2. CJEU Case C-634/21 - SCHUFA Holding v Bundesverwaltungsgericht

**Case Name:** SCHUFA Holding AG v Bundesverwaltungsgericht (Federal Administrative Court, Germany)

**Case Number:** C-634/21

**Judgment Date:** 7 December 2023

**Court:** CJEU (First Chamber)

**Citation:** ECLI:EU:C:2023:957

**Nature:** Preliminary ruling on scope and application of Article 22 GDPR to credit scoring

**Official Locations:**

| Source | URL | Notes |
|--------|-----|-------|
| **CURIA Official Judgment** | `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=282187&doclang=EN` | Full judgment text |
| **CURIA Case List** | `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-634%2F21` | Case summary and documents |
| **EUR-Lex Consolidated Text** | `https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62021CJ0634` | Official EU law database |
| **CURIA Opinion of Advocate General** | `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=271343&doclang=EN` | AG Pikamäe Opinion (16 March 2023) |

**Significance:** First CJEU judgment interpreting Article 22 GDPR on automated decision-making. Landmark decision establishing scope of prohibition.

**Key Holdings:**

1. **Preparatory Acts as Decisions:**
   - Automated credit scoring by third-party agency constitutes a "decision" under Article 22(1)
   - Not limited to final decisions but includes determinative preparatory steps
   - When third-party lenders "draw strongly" on scores, those scores are decisions

2. **Three Conditions Established:**
   - (1) Decision: Exists (automated credit score affects lending)
   - (2) Solely automated: Met (purely algorithmic score generation)
   - (3) Legal/significant effects: Met (affects access to credit, financial circumstances)

3. **Scope of Article 22 Applicability:**
   - Applies to service providers creating scores/profiles used by third parties
   - Not limited to entity making final decision
   - Extends Article 22 obligations upstream in decision chain

4. **Right to Explanation:**
   - Controller must provide meaningful information about how score was calculated
   - Related to right of access under Article 15(1)(h)
   - Scope clarified in subsequent C-203/22 judgment

**Preparatory Opinion by Advocate General Pikamäe (16 March 2023):**
- URL: `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=271343&doclang=EN`
- Recommended broad interpretation of "decision" and "similarly significant effects"
- Established framework for third-party reliance as determinative factor

### 3. CJEU Case C-203/22 - CK v Magistrat der Stadt Wien (Dun & Bradstreet Austria)

**Case Name:** CK v Magistrat der Stadt Wien (represented by Dun & Bradstreet Austria GmbH)

**Case Number:** C-203/22

**Judgment Date:** 26 February 2025

**Court:** CJEU (Grand Chamber)

**Citation:** ECLI:EU:C:2025:131

**Nature:** Preliminary ruling on transparency obligations and right to explanation in automated decision-making; balance between GDPR rights and trade secret protection

**Official Locations:**

| Source | URL | Notes |
|--------|-----|-------|
| **CURIA Official Judgment** | `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=295841&doclang=EN` | Full judgment text |
| **CURIA Case List** | `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-203%2F22` | Case documents |
| **EUR-Lex** | `https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62022CJ0203` | Official consolidated text |
| **CURIA Opinion of Advocate General** | `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=290022&doclang=EN` | AG de la Tour Opinion (12 September 2024) |

**Significance:** Most recent CJEU judgment on ADM; clarifies "meaningful information" standard and resolves tension between transparency rights and trade secret protection.

**Key Holdings:**

1. **Meaningful Information Standard:**
   - Controllers must provide "meaningful information about the logic involved"
   - Means explaining "the procedure and principles actually applied"
   - Goes beyond high-level algorithm categories or statistical abstractions
   - Must enable data subject to understand how decision was reached

2. **Content Requirements:**
   - Explanation must relate to concrete data subject's situation
   - Must identify factors influencing the decision
   - Must facilitate exercise of access and contestation rights
   - Must be understandable without technical expertise requirement

3. **Trade Secrets vs. GDPR Rights:**
   - Trade secret protection does NOT excuse transparency obligations
   - Controllers must find means to explain while protecting legitimate interests
   - Judicial authorities can access proprietary information for rights enforcement
   - DPAs retain authority to investigate algorithmic systems

4. **Right of Access and Challenge:**
   - Data subject access right (Article 15) includes algorithmic transparency
   - Right to contest decision (Article 22(3)) requires substantive explanation
   - Cannot be satisfied by reference to general algorithm descriptions

**Advocate General Opinion (12 September 2024):**
- URL: `https://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=290022&doclang=EN`
- Recommended balancing approach: transparency obligations do not require disclosure of algorithm source code but do require practical explanation of decision logic
