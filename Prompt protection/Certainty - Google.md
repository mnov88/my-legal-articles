# Navigating the Doctrine of Certainty: Enforceability of Variable Scope Service Contracts in a Comparative Legal Context

## Introduction

### Framing the Core Challenge

The modern service economy increasingly demands flexibility. Business models predicated on cloud computing, software-as-a-service (SaaS), and on-demand professional services thrive on their ability to scale resources in response to fluctuating client needs. This commercial imperative for adaptable, demand-driven agreements, however, creates a fundamental tension with one of the oldest and most foundational principles of contract law: the requirement of certainty. For a contract to be valid and enforceable, its essential terms must be clear enough for a court to ascertain the parties' obligations, determine if a breach has occurred, and fashion an appropriate remedy.1 A term that is too vague or ambiguous risks rendering the entire agreement a nullity—an unenforceable "agreement to agree".3

This report addresses the critical legal challenge that arises when this tension is crystallized in a single contractual clause. It examines the enforceability of service agreements where a core obligation—the quantity of service to be provided—is not fixed but is instead variable, contingent on an unpredictable factor.

### Hypothetical Scenario

To ground the analysis in a practical context, this report will use a recurring hypothetical scenario. **Company A**, a technology firm, offers access to a specialized online platform and associated expert support services. To appeal to a wide range of clients, from startups to large enterprises, Company A wishes to market its service not on a fixed-retainer basis but on a flexible, usage-based model. Its proposed standard service agreement includes the following core provision concerning the quantity of service:

> "Company A shall provide the Client with access to a number of expert support hours per month _depending subject to demand_."

This clause, on its face, is fraught with ambiguity. What constitutes "demand"? Who measures it? What are the limits, if any, on the service provider's obligation or the client's entitlement? This report will analyze the legal status of this clause and, by extension, the entire agreement, under the contract law regimes of several key commercial jurisdictions.

### Report Objective and Roadmap

The objective of this report is to provide a detailed, multi-jurisdictional legal analysis of the "subject to demand" clause and, more broadly, the doctrine of certainty as it applies to variable scope service contracts. The analysis will move from foundational principles to a comparative examination of major legal systems, culminating in a practical framework for drafting an enforceable agreement that successfully balances commercial flexibility with legal certainty.

The report is structured as follows:

- **Section 1** establishes the foundational legal principles of certainty in contract formation, introducing the critical distinction between "determined" and "determinable" terms and outlining the high-level differences between the common law and civil law traditions.
    
- **Section 2** examines the common law approach, focusing on the requirement for objective standards in English and United States law.
    
- **Section 3** analyzes the civil law approach, exploring the roles of codified rules, good faith, and judicial intervention in Germany and France.
    
- **Section 4** looks to international instruments, such as the UNIDROIT Principles and the CISG, for guidance on the global consensus regarding certainty in commercial contracts.
    
- **Section 5** synthesizes the preceding legal analysis into an actionable framework, providing concrete drafting strategies and mechanisms to render a variable scope service contract enforceable across these jurisdictions.
    

By navigating these diverse legal landscapes, this report aims to equip business leaders and their counsel with the understanding necessary to structure innovative, flexible service agreements that are commercially attractive and legally robust.

## Section 1: The Bedrock of Contract Formation: The Principle of Certainty

### The Rationale for Certainty

The requirement of certainty is not a mere legal technicality; it is the cornerstone of an enforceable contract.5The law requires clarity in agreements for a profoundly practical reason: a court must be able to understand what the parties have promised each other to perform its essential functions. Without a sufficient degree of certainty, a court cannot objectively determine whether a party has failed to perform its obligations (a breach) or calculate the appropriate compensation (a remedy) for that failure.1 If the terms are so vague or incomplete that the court would have to guess at the parties' intentions or, in effect, write the contract for them, the law concludes that no "meeting of the minds" occurred.6 In such cases, what may appear to be a contract is legally classified as a mere "agreement to agree," which is a legal nullity.3

This principle ensures that parties are only bound to obligations they have clearly understood and accepted. It provides a framework for predictable commercial relationships, reducing the risk of disputes arising from misunderstandings and ensuring that contractual commitments can be relied upon.3

### Essential Terms (Essentialia Negotii)

While not every detail of a contract must be spelled out, the law demands certainty with respect to its essential terms, often referred to by the Latin term _essentialia negotii_. These are the core elements without which the agreement lacks a coherent commercial purpose. Generally, these essential terms include:

1. **The identity of the parties:** The contract must make it clear who is bound by the agreement.7
    
2. **The subject matter:** The agreement must clearly define the goods to be sold or the services to be rendered.7
    
3. **The price or consideration:** The contract must specify the price to be paid or the mechanism for determining it.4 In service contracts, this is inextricably linked to the quantity of the service.
    

In the context of the Company A hypothetical, the "number of hours" of expert support is a fundamental component of both the subject matter and the price. A failure to define this term with sufficient certainty would likely cause the entire contract to fail in the eyes of the law, as it leaves a critical obligation undefined.9

### "Determined vs. Determinable": The Crucial Distinction

The requirement of certainty does not mean that every essential term must be fixed and immutable at the moment the contract is signed. Contract law across jurisdictions makes a crucial distinction between terms that are **determined** and terms that are **determinable**.10

- A **determined** term is one that is explicitly stated and fixed in the contract. For example, "Company A will provide 100 hours of support per month for a fixed fee of $10,000."
    
- A **determinable** term is one that is not explicitly stated as a fixed value but can be ascertained with certainty by reference to a mechanism, formula, or standard provided within the contract itself, without the need for further negotiation between the parties.10 For example, "The price for the goods shall be the price listed on the London Metal Exchange at the close of business on the date of delivery."
    

This distinction is the central pivot upon which the enforceability of variable scope contracts turns. A clause like "depending subject to demand" is facially uncertain and, if left as is, would likely be deemed an unenforceable agreement to agree. The legal challenge, therefore, is not to eliminate the variability desired by Company A, but to construct a contractual framework that transforms the variable "number of hours" from a vague, undetermined concept into a legally acceptable, _determinable_ one.11 The contract itself must provide the key to unlock the precise obligation when the time for performance arrives.

### The Common Law vs. Civil Law Divide

The approach to achieving certainty and interpreting contracts differs significantly between the world's two major legal traditions: common law and civil law. These differences shape how a clause like "subject to demand" would be analyzed and what level of contractual detail is required.

- **Common Law Jurisdictions (e.g., UK, US, Australia):**
    
    - **Source of Law:** Primarily based on judicial precedent (_stare decisis_), where past court decisions are binding.13
        
    - **Contractual Detail:** Parties are expected to create comprehensive, detailed contracts that explicitly state all terms. Very few terms are implied by law, reflecting the principle of "freedom of contract".15This often results in significantly longer agreements.
        
    - **Interpretation:** Courts apply an "objective theory of contract," meaning they interpret the contract based on the plain meaning of the words used, as understood by a reasonable person, rather than trying to ascertain the parties' subjective intentions.7
        
    - **Consideration:** A contract is generally only enforceable if there is "consideration"—a bargained-for exchange of value between the parties.13
        
- **Civil Law Jurisdictions (e.g., Germany, France, Spain):**
    
    - **Source of Law:** Primarily based on comprehensive, codified legal systems, such as the German Civil Code (BGB) or the French Civil Code.13 Judicial decisions serve to interpret the code but are not, in theory, a primary source of law themselves.
        
    - **Contractual Detail:** Contracts can be shorter because the civil code provides numerous default rules (_ius dispositivum_) that automatically apply unless the parties expressly agree otherwise.15
        
    - **Interpretation:** While objective interpretation is important, courts may also give more weight to the parties' presumed subjective intent and overarching principles like good faith, even if it requires departing from the literal text.13
        
    - **Consideration:** The common law doctrine of consideration is not a requirement for contract formation.13
        

These foundational differences mean that the path to creating an enforceable variable scope contract will involve different strategies and risk considerations depending on the governing law. In common law, the focus will be on building explicit, objective mechanisms into the contract. In civil law, while such mechanisms are valuable, the drafter must also be mindful of mandatory codified principles like good faith and fairness, which can override the parties' express terms.

## Section 2: The Common Law Approach: Objective Standards and Judicial Reluctance to "Make a Bargain"

The common law tradition is built on the foundational principle that courts exist to enforce the bargain that the parties have made for themselves, not to create a bargain for them.4 This philosophy directly informs the doctrine of certainty. When faced with an ambiguous or incomplete term, a common law court will not invent a solution. Instead, it will search for an objective standard or mechanism, either within the contract or in the surrounding commercial context, to give the term meaning. If no such objective benchmark can be found, the term—and potentially the entire contract—will be deemed void for uncertainty.

### 2.1. English Law: Upholding Commercial Bargains Within Limits

English contract law displays a pragmatic, pro-commerce inclination. Courts are generally reluctant to strike down commercial agreements, particularly where the parties have demonstrated an intention to be bound by commencing performance.18 The judicial role is to give legal effect to what the parties have agreed, recognizing that businesspeople may not always record their agreements with "lawyerly precision".18 The overarching test is whether the court can apply a "reasonable construction" to the disputed term to make the agreement work.4 However, this judicial willingness has firm limits, as demonstrated by key precedents.

A foundational case, _Hillas & Co Ltd v Arcos Ltd_ UKHL 2, illustrates the court's willingness to look to context. The House of Lords upheld an option to purchase timber of "fair specification." Standing alone, this term appears vague. However, the court gave it meaning by looking at the context of the previous dealings between the parties under the same contract, which had established a clear pattern and understanding of what constituted a "fair specification" of timber. The court effectively used the parties' own past conduct as an objective dictionary to define the ambiguous term.4

Conversely, _Scammell and Nephew Ltd v Ouston_ AC 251 demonstrates the boundary of this principle. An agreement to purchase a van "on hire purchase terms" was held to be unenforceable. Unlike the term in _Hillas_, there was no single, objective standard for what constituted "hire purchase terms." The phrase could encompass a wide variety of different financial arrangements with varying interest rates, durations, and obligations. The court had no external or contextual benchmark to refer to and therefore concluded that the parties had not reached a concluded agreement on an essential term.4

More recently, the case of _Baird Textile Holdings Ltd v M&S plc_ EWCA Civ 274 reinforced the need for certainty in core obligations. Baird had supplied clothing to M&S for 30 years without a formal contract. When M&S terminated the relationship, Baird argued that a contract should be implied, including a term requiring reasonable notice of termination. The Court of Appeal rejected this argument, holding that the absence of certainty regarding price and quantity—which were decided on a seasonal basis—was so fundamental that it was impossible to imply an overarching contract that could support a notice period.4

Applying these principles to the Company A hypothetical, an English court would almost certainly find the clause "depending subject to demand," in isolation, to be void for uncertainty. It is a quintessential "agreement to agree".3 It provides no objective benchmark by which a court could measure "demand" or determine the extent of Company A's obligation. To be enforceable under English law, the contract would need to be supplemented with mechanisms that provide this missing objectivity. For example, it could link "demand" to the client's historical usage patterns, specify a procedural mechanism for ordering and accepting hours, or provide for determination by a neutral third party in case of disagreement. Without such a framework, the clause lacks the certainty required for enforcement.

### 2.2. United States Law: The "Reasonably Certain" Standard

United States contract law operates under a similar conceptual framework to English law but is often viewed as more flexible and liberal in its willingness to find an enforceable contract, particularly in commercial contexts. This flexibility is heavily influenced by the philosophy of the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC).

The guiding principle in US law is the **Objective Theory of Contract**. This doctrine holds that a party's intent to be bound is determined not by their subjective, internal thoughts but by the objective, reasonable interpretation of their words and actions.16 A contract is considered valid if its terms are **"reasonably certain"**.2 The Restatement (Second) of Contracts, a highly influential legal treatise, defines this standard by stating that the terms are reasonably certain if they "provide a basis for determining the existence of a breach and for giving an appropriate remedy".2 This formulation focuses on the practical ability of a court to enforce the agreement.

While the UCC governs the sale of goods, not services, its approach to contract formation has had a profound influence on all areas of US commercial law. **UCC § 2-204(3)** famously states: "Even though one or more terms are left open a contract for sale does not fail for indefiniteness if the parties have intended to make a contract and there is a reasonably certain basis for giving an appropriate remedy".2 This provision signals a clear departure from a rigid, formalistic approach. It prioritizes the parties' manifest intention to be bound over the completeness of their written terms. For instance, **UCC § 2-305** explicitly allows parties to conclude a contract for sale even if the price is not settled, in which case the price becomes a "reasonable price at the time for delivery".2

Applying this framework to the Company A hypothetical, a US court would first look for evidence of the parties' intent to be bound. If Company A and its client have started to perform under the agreement—for example, the client has paid an initial fee and Company A has provided some support hours—a court would be highly motivated to find an enforceable contract.2

However, the "reasonably certain basis for a remedy" requirement remains. The phrase "depending subject to demand" is still problematic because it offers no clear standard. A US court would be more willing than its English counterpart to look at contextual evidence, such as the parties' pre-contractual negotiations, trade customs, or a course of performance, to give meaning to the term "demand".7 Nevertheless, if no such objective basis can be found, the contract may still fail for indefiniteness.21 The most effective way to ensure enforceability in the US is the same as in the UK: to build the objective criteria for determining the quantity of service directly into the agreement. This might include formulas based on user activity, clear procedures for placing orders, or defined tiers of service.7 This removes the need for the court to supply a missing term and provides the "reasonably certain basis" for a remedy that the law requires.

The consistent theme across these common law jurisdictions is that while courts will strive to uphold commercial agreements, they will not do so by inventing terms out of thin air. The enforceability of a variable term like "subject to demand" is directly proportional to the objectivity of the mechanism provided for its determination. Subjectivity, which invites a court to impose its own judgment on what the parties _should have_agreed, is the enemy of certainty and, by extension, enforceability. The path to a valid contract lies in providing the court with a verifiable, objective roadmap to follow.

## Section 3: The Civil Law Approach: Codified Rules, Good faith, and Judicial Intervention

Civil law systems approach the problem of contractual certainty from a different philosophical starting point. Rather than relying solely on the explicit text of the agreement as interpreted through precedent, civil law jurisdictions are built upon comprehensive legal codes that provide a host of default rules and overarching principles. These principles—such as good faith, equity, and fairness—are not merely interpretive aids but are powerful legal duties that can shape, supplement, and in some cases, override the express terms of a contract. This grants judges a more interventionist role, allowing them to review and even modify contractual terms to ensure they comply with the code's mandatory principles.

### 3.1. German Law: The Power of Subsequent Determination

The German Civil Code (_Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch_, or BGB) provides a highly structured and logical framework for contract law. For a contract to be formed, there must be a consensus on the _essentialia negotii_ (essential terms), which, for a service contract, would include the nature of the service and the remuneration.22 An offer must be precise enough that it can be accepted with a simple "yes".22

A key feature of German law that is directly relevant to the Company A hypothetical is the explicit permission for **subsequent determination** of performance, codified in **§§ 315-317 of the BGB**. These provisions allow parties to agree that an essential term, such as the quantity of a service, will be determined at a later date by one of the parties or by a neutral third party.11 This stands in stark contrast to the common law's deep suspicion of such arrangements, which are often dismissed as "agreements to agree."

Under **§ 315 BGB**, if the right to determine performance is granted to one of the contracting parties (e.g., Company A determining the hours "subject to demand"), the law imposes a crucial constraint: the determination must be made "in an equitable manner" (_nach billigem Ermessen_), which translates to "according to reasonable discretion".11 This is not a subjective standard. The party's decision is subject to full judicial review. If a court finds the determination to be inequitable or unfair, it is not binding, and the court itself can determine the performance by its own judgment.11 This provides a powerful, built-in check on any potential abuse of discretion.

However, this flexibility is sharply curtailed by Germany's strict rules on standard terms and conditions (_Allgemeine Geschäftsbedingungen_, or AGB). These rules, found in **§§ 305-310 BGB**, were originally designed for consumer protection but apply with nearly equal force in business-to-business (B2B) transactions.23 Under **§ 307 BGB**, a clause in a standard contract is invalid if it places the other party at an "unreasonable disadvantage contrary to the requirements of good faith".24 A clause granting one party the unilateral right to determine a core contractual obligation, such as the quantity of service, without clear, objective, and fair criteria, would face a very high risk of being invalidated under this test.25 German courts have repeatedly struck down unilateral price or service change clauses in B2C contracts for being vague and unfair, a logic that can readily be extended to B2B standard terms.26

Applying this to Company A, the "subject to demand" clause could be structured to be enforceable under German law, but the path is narrow. If the contract is individually negotiated, relying on § 315 BGB could be a viable strategy, as Company A's determination of hours would be subject to the backstop of judicial review for equitableness. However, if the clause is part of Company A's standard service agreement (which is far more likely for a scalable business model), it is highly vulnerable. To survive AGB scrutiny, the clause would need to be carefully balanced. It would likely need to define "demand" with reference to objective criteria, establish a transparent process for its determination, and perhaps include a reciprocal obligation on Company A to scale up resources if demand demonstrably increases, thus avoiding an "unreasonable disadvantage" to the client.

### 3.2. French Law: Good Faith and the Duty to Inform

French contract law underwent a major overhaul with the **2016 reform of the Civil Code**, which aimed to codify decades of case law, enhance legal certainty, and make French law more attractive for international commerce.27 The reformed code places a heavy emphasis on overarching principles of fairness and cooperation throughout the contractual relationship.

A central pillar of the new law is the expanded **duty of good faith** (_bonne foi_). **Article 1104** of the Civil Code now explicitly states that "contracts must be negotiated, formed and performed in good faith".27 This is a public policy provision that cannot be excluded by the parties. This duty imposes a positive obligation on the parties to act honestly and fairly not just during the life of the contract, but during its formation.

This general duty is supplemented by a specific **pre-contractual duty to inform** under **Article 1112-1**. This article provides that a party who knows information whose importance is "decisive for the consent of the other" must disclose it, provided the other party is legitimately ignorant of the information or trusts its counterparty.29In the Company A hypothetical, this could be interpreted as requiring Company A to proactively and transparently explain to the client how it defines and measures "demand," what factors influence service availability, and what the practical limitations of the service are. A failure to do so could lead to the contract being annulled or give rise to a claim for damages.

Furthermore, French law grants significant power to judges. **Article 1171** allows a court to declare a clause in a "standard-form contract" (_contrat d'adhésion_) to be void if it creates a "significant imbalance" between the rights and obligations of the parties.27 A clause that gives Company A unfettered discretion to determine the level of service "subject to demand" could easily be seen as creating such an imbalance, particularly if the client is paying a recurring fee.

Finally, French case law, especially in the context of technology and other complex service contracts, has developed a strong **duty to advise** (_obligation de conseil_).32 This doctrine requires the professional or more knowledgeable party (the supplier) to take active steps to understand the client's needs and to advise them on the suitability of the proposed solution. The supplier must be proactive in seeking information from the client to ensure the service is fit for purpose.32

In applying these principles to Company A's clause, a French court would analyze it through the prism of good faith, transparency, and balance. The clause "depending subject to demand" would not be assessed in a vacuum. The court would ask: Did Company A act in good faith during negotiations? Did it fulfill its duty to inform the client about the mechanics of "demand"? Does the clause, in the context of the entire agreement, create a significant imbalance? Did Company A fulfill its duty to advise the client on whether a variable service model was appropriate for their needs? If Company A used the clause to arbitrarily restrict service or failed to be transparent about its operation, a French court would have ample grounds under the Civil Code to nullify the clause or even the entire contract. Enforceability would depend on demonstrating a collaborative and transparent approach to defining and managing the variable service level.

In essence, while common law systems demand that objectivity be explicitly engineered into the contract's text, civil law systems impose it from the outside through mandatory duties of good faith and fairness. The risk for a drafter in a common law jurisdiction is that the contract is deemed void for uncertainty. The risk in a civil law jurisdiction is that a seemingly valid clause is later modified or nullified by a court for being inequitable or unfair.

## Section 4: International Instruments as a Guide: The "Determinable" Standard in Practice

In an increasingly globalized commercial environment, contracts often cross borders, involving parties from both common law and civil law traditions. In this context, international instruments like the UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG) and the UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts (PICC) provide valuable insight. These documents were drafted as a synthesis of different legal traditions and represent a modern consensus on best practices in international trade law. Their approach to contractual certainty signals a pragmatic, pro-commerce trend that favors upholding agreements wherever possible.

### The UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG)

Although the CISG applies to the international sale of goods, not services, its principles are highly influential and often looked to by analogy in international services disputes. The CISG's approach to contract formation reflects the "determined or determinable" standard. **Article 14** of the CISG requires that for a proposal to constitute a valid offer, it must be "sufficiently definite." It achieves this standard if it "indicates the goods and expressly or implicitly fixes or makes provision for determining the quantity and the price." This language explicitly endorses the idea that a contract can be valid even if terms are not fixed, so long as a mechanism for determining them is in place.

Notably, while **Article 55** of the CISG provides a gap-filling rule for price (stating that if the price is not determined or determinable, the parties are considered to have impliedly made reference to the price generally charged for such goods), there is no equivalent provision for quantity. This reinforces the view, common across legal systems, that quantity is such a fundamental element of the bargain that the parties themselves must provide the means for its determination. The convention will not create a quantity term for them. This underscores that for Company A, while some flexibility on price might be tolerated, the mechanism for determining the quantity of service hours is a non-negotiable prerequisite for forming a valid international contract.

### The UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts (PICC)

The PICC are a set of non-binding rules and principles intended to establish a balanced and modern framework for international commercial contracts. They are frequently chosen by parties as the governing law for international arbitration and are considered a persuasive restatement of global commercial law. The PICC strongly embody the principle of _favor contractus_—the principle of interpreting the law in a way that preserves the contract.33

**Article 2.1.2** of the PICC states that a contract is concluded if the parties intend to be bound and "the terms are sufficiently definite." Like the CISG, the PICC embrace flexibility. **Article 5.1.7** provides a robust gap-filler for price, stating that where the price is not fixed, the parties are treated as having referred to "the price generally charged at the time of the conclusion of the contract for such performance in comparable circumstances in the trade concerned or, if no such price is available, a reasonable price".11

Crucially, the PICC integrate principles from both common and civil law traditions. They emphasize the importance of good faith and fair dealing in international trade (Article 1.7) and allow for terms to be implied based on the nature and purpose of the contract, practices established between the parties, and usages (Article 5.1.2). This approach empowers courts and arbitral tribunals to look beyond a rigid, textual analysis and to uphold agreements that make commercial sense, filling gaps where necessary based on objective standards of reasonableness and good faith.33

The approach of these international instruments provides a clear message for a business like Company A operating in the international market. A modern commercial contract with a variable scope is likely to be viewed as enforceable by an international tribunal, provided it reflects a clear intention by the parties to be legally bound and contains a rational, commercially reasonable basis for determining their obligations. The global trend is to move away from overly formalistic formation requirements and towards an analysis that validates the parties' commercial bargain. The "subject to demand" clause, if supported by mechanisms that make it determinable and operate in good faith, aligns with this modern, pragmatic approach to international contract law.

## Section 5: A Practical Framework for Drafting an Enforceable Variable Service Contract

The preceding analysis demonstrates that while common law and civil law systems approach the doctrine of certainty from different theoretical perspectives, they converge on a practical outcome: a variable service obligation is enforceable if the contract successfully transforms the term from a subjective, uncertain concept into an objective, determinable one. This section synthesizes that analysis into a concrete framework of drafting strategies and mechanisms that Company A can use to create a robust, cross-jurisdictionally enforceable service agreement.

### 5.1. From Ambiguity to Determinability: Defining the Undefined

The primary task is to cure the ambiguity inherent in the phrase "depending subject to demand." This requires replacing vague language with clear, precise, and measurable criteria.34 The goal is to provide a court or arbitrator with a clear, self-contained method for ascertaining the parties' obligations without resorting to further negotiation or speculation.35

Three primary mechanisms can be employed to achieve this determinability:

1. **Objective Metric Linkage:** The most effective method is to tie the variable quantity of service hours directly to an external, verifiable, and objective metric that is controlled or generated by the client's own activities. This creates a direct, causal link between the client's need ("demand") and the provider's obligation.
    
    - **_Example Clause:_** _"Provider shall make available to Client a quantity of Service Hours per calendar month calculated in accordance with the formula set forth in Schedule A. The formula shall be based on the number of Client's monthly active users ('MAUs') as measured by Client's internal, authenticated user logs, which shall be made available to Provider for verification purposes upon reasonable request."_
        
    - This approach is strong because it relies on data that is objective and, in many cases, within the client's control, making it difficult for either party to dispute the resulting calculation in bad faith.36
        
2. **Formalized Procedural Mechanisms:** An alternative or supplementary approach is to create a clear, contractually defined process for forecasting, requesting, and confirming the quantity of service. This method replaces a single, vague standard with a series of clear, procedural obligations.
    
    - **_Example Clause:_** _"Client shall provide Provider with a non-binding, good-faith forecast of its required Service Hours for the upcoming calendar quarter no later than fifteen (15) days prior to the start of said quarter. Based on this forecast, Client shall place firm, binding orders for Service Hours on a weekly basis, no later than the Wednesday of the preceding week. Provider shall be obligated to fulfill all firm orders placed in accordance with this procedure, subject to the Maximum Commitment defined in Section X."_
        
    - This mechanism provides certainty on a rolling basis and creates a clear paper trail of the parties' agreements, making it easy to determine if a breach has occurred.35
        
3. **Third-Party Determination:** To resolve potential disputes over the interpretation of "demand" or the application of a formula, the contract can delegate the final determination to a neutral third-party expert. This provides a built-in dispute resolution mechanism that avoids litigation.
    
    - **_Example Clause:_** _"In the event of a dispute between the parties as to the required level of Service Hours in any given period, the matter shall be referred to a mutually agreed-upon independent expert from an accredited IT consultancy firm. The expert shall act as an expert and not as an arbitrator, and their determination of the required Service Hours, based on the criteria set forth in this Agreement and prevailing industry standards, shall be final and binding on both parties."_
        
    - This approach is particularly useful where the metric for demand is complex and may require specialized knowledge to assess fairly.7
        

### 5.2. Structuring the Commercial Relationship: Managing Risk and Expectations

Beyond defining the variable term, the overall commercial structure of the agreement must be designed to manage the risks and expectations inherent in a flexible model.

- **Minimum and Maximum Commitments (Floors and Ceilings):** This is arguably the most critical risk-mitigation tool for both parties. It creates a "band of certainty" within which the variability can operate. The provider is guaranteed a minimum level of revenue and can plan resources accordingly, while the client is guaranteed a maximum level of cost and service availability. This bounding of the obligation is crucial for demonstrating that the parties' commitments, while flexible, are not limitless or illusory.36
    
    - **_Example Clause:_** _"Notwithstanding any other provision of this Agreement, in any calendar month, Provider shall be obligated to provide, and Client shall be obligated to purchase, no fewer than fifty (50) Service Hours ('Minimum Commitment') and no more than five hundred (500) Service Hours ('Maximum Commitment'). Provider shall have no obligation to provide Service Hours in excess of the Maximum Commitment, and Client shall be obligated to pay for the Minimum Commitment whether or not the hours are used."_
        
- **Tiered or Usage-Based Pricing:** The payment structure should logically follow the variable service model. A tiered pricing structure can incentivize higher usage while providing cost predictability.
    
    - **_Example Clause:_** _"Fees for Service Hours shall be calculated monthly based on the following tiers: (a) Hours 1-100: $150 per hour; (b) Hours 101-300: $125 per hour; (c) Hours 301-500: $100 per hour."_
        
    - This structure directly links cost to consumption, reinforcing the commercial logic of the agreement.36
        
- **Service Level Agreements (SLAs):** A detailed SLA shifts the contractual focus from the mere _quantity_ of hours to the _quality_ of the service delivered, which is often the client's primary concern. A robust SLA provides an independent set of measurable obligations that are enforceable regardless of fluctuations in demand.
    
    - **Performance Metrics:** Define key performance indicators (KPIs) such as platform uptime (e.g., 99.9%), response times for support tickets based on priority (e.g., 1 hour for "Urgent," 8 business hours for "Normal"), and resolution targets.38
        
    - **Communication Protocols:** Specify how and when the provider will communicate about maintenance, outages, and the status of service requests.41
        
    - **Service Credits:** Include a pre-agreed remedy for SLA failures, typically in the form of a credit against the next month's invoice. This provides a clear and certain remedy for a breach of the quality obligation, strengthening the overall enforceability of the contract.41

## Conclusion

### Synthesis of Findings

The central challenge posed by a service contract with a variable scope, such as one offering hours "depending subject to demand," is to reconcile the commercial desire for flexibility with the fundamental legal requirement for certainty. This report's comparative analysis of common law and civil law jurisdictions reveals that while the theoretical pathways differ, they lead to a convergent practical conclusion: a variable obligation is enforceable, but only if the contract provides a clear and objective framework for its determination.

In **common law systems** like the UK and the US, the principle that courts will not "make a bargain for the parties" is paramount. Enforceability hinges on the contract containing an objective mechanism—be it a formula, a clear procedure, or reference to an external standard—that allows a court to ascertain the parties' obligations without resorting to subjective interpretation. The absence of such a mechanism renders the agreement an unenforceable "agreement to agree."

In **civil law systems** like Germany and France, the analysis is filtered through the lens of overarching duties and codified principles. While these systems are more tolerant of granting discretion to one party, that discretion is never absolute. It is constrained by mandatory legal duties of good faith, equitableness, and transparency. A variable clause will be upheld only if it operates fairly and does not create an unreasonable disadvantage or significant imbalance to the detriment of the other party.

Ultimately, both legal traditions seek to prevent the same outcome: one party being subject to the arbitrary and unpredictable will of the other. Common law achieves this by demanding that constraints be explicitly written into the contract; civil law achieves it by imposing constraints from the legal code itself.

### Final Recommendations for Company A

To create a robust and globally enforceable service agreement based on a variable, demand-driven model, Company A should adopt a multi-layered drafting strategy that incorporates the most effective elements from both legal traditions.

1. **Define "Demand" Objectively:** The core ambiguity of the phrase "subject to demand" must be eliminated. The most resilient strategy is to link the quantity of service hours to a verifiable, objective client-side metric, such as active users, transaction volumes, or data storage usage. This transforms the obligation from a subjective assessment into a calculable outcome.
    
2. **Bound the Obligation:** The single most effective risk-mitigation tool is the inclusion of minimum and maximum commitments. A floor and a ceiling on the monthly service hours provide a framework of certainty for both parties, ensuring the provider has predictable minimum revenue and the client has a predictable maximum cost and guaranteed level of service. This structure is highly likely to be found sufficiently certain in all jurisdictions.
    
3. **Establish Clear Processes:** The contract should detail a formal process for forecasting, ordering, and accepting service hours. This procedural clarity provides evidence of a "meeting of the minds" on a rolling basis and simplifies the determination of a breach.
    
4. **Focus on Performance Quality via SLAs:** A comprehensive Service Level Agreement (SLA) with clear KPIs and service credits for non-performance should be a central component of the agreement. This shifts the focus from the potentially contentious issue of quantity to the more critical issue of service quality, providing the client with a meaningful and certain remedy for performance failures.
    

### Concluding Thought

The evolution of commerce continually pushes the boundaries of traditional legal doctrines. The rise of flexible, "as-a-service" business models is a prime example of this dynamic. While a clause like "depending subject to demand" may seem at odds with the classical requirement of contractual certainty, it is not an insurmountable obstacle. By understanding the underlying rationale of the doctrine of certainty and employing thoughtful, precise drafting techniques that build in objectivity, procedural clarity, and mutual fairness, a company can successfully bridge the gap between commercial innovation and legal enforceability, creating a modern service contract that is both flexible and firm.
